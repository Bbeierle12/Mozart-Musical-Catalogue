Biography

George Frideric Handel (1685-1759) was a German-British Baroque composer renowned for his operas, oratorios, anthems, concerti grossi, and organ concertos. Born in Halle, Germany, Handel spent most of his career in London, becoming a naturalized British subject in 1727.

Handel's music is characterized by grand ceremonial works, dramatic power, and melodic invention. He was a master of borrowing and reworking musical material, transforming it into works of stunning originality. His most famous composition, Messiah, remains one of the most frequently performed choral works in Western music.

Unlike his contemporary J.S. Bach, who worked primarily in church and court positions, Handel was an independent entrepreneur who wrote for public audiences. His operas dominated London's theatres in the 1720s and 1730s, and when Italian opera fell out of favor, he successfully transitioned to English oratorios.

Handel's influence on subsequent composers was enormous, particularly on Mozart, Beethoven, and the development of the English choral tradition. He died in London in 1759 and was buried with full state honors in Westminster Abbey.

Oratorios (HWV 47-75)

Handel composed 29 oratorios, establishing the English oratorio as a major genre. These dramatic works on biblical themes were performed in theatres rather than churches, making sacred music accessible to the public.

About Messiah

Handel's Messiah (HWV 56) was composed in an astonishing 24 days in August-September 1741. It premiered in Dublin on April 13, 1742, and has been performed continuously ever since. The work is unique among Handel's oratorios in focusing on meditation rather than dramatic narrative.

Famous movements: "Comfort Ye," "Every Valley," "For Unto Us a Child is Born," and the "Hallelujah" chorus.

Operas (HWV 1-42)

Handel composed 42 operas, primarily in Italian, dominating London's operatic scene for over two decades. His operas feature virtuoso arias, dramatic recitatives, and compelling characterizations.

Handel's Operatic Career

Handel's first London opera, Rinaldo (1711), was an immediate success, featuring elaborate stage effects and the famous aria "Lascia ch'io pianga." His operatic masterpiece, Giulio Cesare (1724), remains popular today for its dramatic power and beautiful arias.

Orchestral Works

Water Music (HWV 348-350)

Three orchestral suites composed for King George I's trip on the River Thames in 1717. The music was so successful that the king requested it to be played three times during the journey.

Concerti Grossi Op. 6 (HWV 312-323)

Twelve concerti grossi composed in 1739, representing Handel's mature instrumental style. These works follow the Italian concerto grosso tradition established by Corelli.

Organ Concertos (HWV 289-311)

Handel composed 23 organ concertos, primarily to be performed between acts of his oratorios. He often improvised the solo parts in performance.

Music for the Royal Fireworks (HWV 351)

1749 • D major

Composed for a massive fireworks display in London's Green Park celebrating the Peace of Aix-la-Chapelle. The rehearsal drew an audience of 12,000 people!

Scoring: 24 oboes, 12 bassoons, 9 trumpets, 9 horns, 3 pairs of timpani

Vocal Works & Anthems

Coronation Anthems (1727)

Four anthems composed for the coronation of King George II and Queen Caroline. Zadok the Priest has been performed at every British coronation since.

Zadok the Priest (HWV 246a)

1727 • D major • 6 minutes

The most famous of the Coronation Anthems, featuring a long orchestral introduction building to the powerful choral entrance: "Zadok the Priest and Nathan the Prophet anointed Solomon King."

First Performance: Westminster Abbey, October 11, 1727

Cultural Impact: Adapted as the UEFA Champions League anthem
