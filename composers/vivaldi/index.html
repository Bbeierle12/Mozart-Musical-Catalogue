Biography

Antonio Lucio Vivaldi (1678-1741) was an Italian Baroque composer, virtuoso violinist, teacher, and Catholic priest. Born in Venice, he was ordained in 1703 but ceased celebrating Mass shortly after due to health reasons. His distinctive red hair earned him the nickname "Il Prete Rosso" (The Red Priest).

Vivaldi spent most of his career at the Ospedale della Pietà, a home for abandoned children where he taught violin and composed music for the all-female music ensemble. The talented students performed his compositions in concerts that attracted visitors from across Europe. This unique environment allowed Vivaldi to experiment with innovative instrumental combinations and virtuosic techniques.

He is recognized as one of the greatest Baroque composers, known particularly for his concertos. His most famous work, The Four Seasons, is among the most popular pieces of Baroque music and pioneered programmatic music—compositions that tell stories or depict scenes. Vivaldi composed over 500 concertos, establishing the three-movement fast-slow-fast structure and ritornello form that became standard.

His music was highly influential on Johann Sebastian Bach, who transcribed several of Vivaldi's concertos for keyboard instruments. Despite international fame during his lifetime, Vivaldi died in poverty in Vienna in 1741 and was buried in a simple grave. His music was largely forgotten until the 20th century revival of interest in Baroque music.

The Four Seasons (Le quattro stagioni)

The Four Seasons is a set of four violin concertos from the larger collection Il cimento dell'armonia e dell'inventione (The Contest between Harmony and Invention), Op. 8, published in 1725. These revolutionary works are among the earliest and most detailed examples of program music—compositions that tell stories.

About The Four Seasons

Each concerto is accompanied by a sonnet (possibly written by Vivaldi himself) that describes the scenes depicted in the music. The sonnets are divided into three parts, corresponding to the three movements of each concerto. Vivaldi went further by indicating specific passages in the music that illustrate particular lines of the sonnets.

Dedication: Bohemian Count Václav Morzin

Published: Amsterdam, 1725 by Michel-Charles Le Cène

Concertos (Over 500 Works)

Vivaldi was the most prolific concerto composer of the Baroque era, composing over 500 concertos for various instruments. He established the three-movement structure (fast-slow-fast) and ritornello form that became the standard for instrumental concertos.

L'estro armonico, Op. 3 (1711)

This groundbreaking collection of 12 concertos established the Italian concerto style across Europe. Many were transcribed by J.S. Bach for keyboard instruments.

La stravaganza, Op. 4 (1714)

Twelve violin concertos dedicated to Venetian nobleman Vettor Delfin, showcasing Vivaldi's virtuosic writing and nicknamed "The Extravagance."

Sacred Vocal Music (60+ Works)

Vivaldi composed extensive sacred music for the Ospedale della Pietà, including masses, motets, oratorios, and psalm settings. These works feature brilliant vocal writing adapted to the talented female musicians of the institution.

Gloria in D major, RV 589

Vivaldi's Most Famous Sacred Work

The Gloria in D major (RV 589) is one of the most frequently performed Baroque choral works. Composed around 1715 for the Ospedale della Pietà, it features festive orchestration with trumpets and oboes, brilliant choral writing, and beautiful solo movements.

Stabat Mater, RV 596

F minor • 1712 • 18 minutes

A deeply expressive solo motet for alto voice depicting Mary's sorrow at the cross. This is one of Vivaldi's most moving sacred works, featuring chromatic harmonies and intense emotional expression. The work is divided into nine brief movements, each exploring different aspects of grief and devotion.

Operas (49 Works, ~20 Survive)

Vivaldi composed nearly 50 operas between 1713 and 1739, though many are now lost. These works were popular in Venice and toured across Europe. Vivaldi not only composed but also served as impresario, organizing productions and managing opera companies.

Vivaldi's Operatic Career

While primarily remembered for his instrumental works, Vivaldi devoted significant energy to opera composition and production. His operas featured elaborate arias showcasing vocal virtuosity, dramatic recitatives, and colorful orchestration. The rediscovery and revival of his operas in recent decades has revealed the dramatic power of these works.

Chamber Music (90+ Works)

Vivaldi composed numerous sonatas and chamber works, primarily for violin but also for other instruments. These intimate works showcase his melodic invention and understanding of instrumental character.

Featured Chamber Works

Sonata for Violin and Continuo, Op. 2 No. 12 "La Follia" (RV 63)

D minor • 1705 • 11 minutes

A set of variations on the famous "La Folia" theme (a Renaissance chord progression used by many composers). This work demonstrates virtuosic violin technique through 20 variations ranging from lyrical to dazzling. It remains one of the most popular violin showpieces from the Baroque era.
